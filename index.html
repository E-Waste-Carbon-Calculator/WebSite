Welcome to the E-waste Carbon Calculator, a specialized tool designed to comprehensive assessments of greenhouse gas emissions linked to waste electrical and electronic equipment (WEEE or e-waste) in Hong Kong. This Calculator empowers producers, retailers, and consumers of Regulated Electrical and Electronic Equipment (REE) in the region to gain a deeper understanding of their local environmental impact and promote sustainable practices in e- waste management.

Understanding E-waste in Hong Kong

Over 70,000 tonnes of e-waste are generated in Hong Kong each year, yet only 32% is recycled at WEEE · PARK.

  • What is E-waste?
    Electronic waste, or e-waste, refers to discarded electrical and electronic equipment that is no longer useful to its owners. In general, the terms “WEEE” and “e-waste” are used synonymously in accordance with the EU WEEE Directive.
  • Where Does All This E-waste go?
    • Export
      Hong Kong used to export its e-waste to developing countries for recycling or reuse; however, starting in 1992, international regulations became more stringent, such as those outlined in the Basel Convention, restricting the global trade of hazardous waste and compelling Hong Kong to manage its e-waste locally.
    • Hong Kong E-waste Management
      To address this challenge, Hong Kong implemented the Producer Responsibility Scheme on Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WPRS) in 2018 to ensure proper recycling and disposal of e-waste at WEEE · PARK.

The WPRS regulates the disposal of key appliances, including air conditioners, refrigerators, washing machines, televisions, computers, printers, scanners, and monitors. Effective from 1 July 2024, the scheme will expand to cover additional appliances, such as larger refrigerators, high-capacity washing machines, tumble dryers, and dehumidifiers, ensuring greater oversight of e-waste recycling.
  • E-Waste Contributes to Climate Change
    The lifecycle of Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment (WEEE) begins with the extraction of raw materials from the environment and concludes with its disposal. Throughout this process, greenhouse gases (GHG) are inevitably emitted to the atmosphere. The disposal method significantly impacts the level of these emissions, with improper disposal—will result in substantial GHG releases.

Improper e-waste disposal emits greenhouse gases through the burning of appliances to extract valuable materials like copper, which will release carbon dioxide (CO2).

Also, appliances such as refrigerators, air conditioners, and dehumidifiers contain refrigerants like chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs). These substances are extremely powerful greenhouse gases. When such appliances are improperly dismantled or dumped without recovering the refrigerants, these gases can leak into the atmosphere, contributing to global warming.
